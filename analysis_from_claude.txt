  ---
  Assessment of checker.py

  No — it is not thorough enough for 100% textual accuracy. It has five structural weaknesses:

  Weaknesses in the existing checker

  1. Samples only 5 words per verse at fixed positional indices (0%, 25%, 50%, 75%, 100%). Any error falling between those positions is invisible. For a 30-word verse, 25 words are never examined.
  2. Positional index comparison is semantically broken. If two extra words are injected at position 0, every subsequent sampled word shifts — turning one real error into five false positives, or
  masking a real error entirely. (The original checker actually does this on D&C 135:4, flagging yes != slaughter and it != upon as mismatches when the text is actually correct — the paragraph break
   mid-verse shifted all subsequent indices.)
  3. Strips <h1> tags but keeps their text content. re.sub(r"<[^>]+>", "", text) removes <h1> and </h1> but leaves "Genesis 2" visible as plain words. My checker strips <h1>...</h1> blocks
  wholesale.
  4. No verse number validation. <sub>3</sub> could appear where <sub>7</sub> belongs and the checker would never notice.
  5. No chapter heading validation. Missing <h1> entries are invisible.
  6. Punctuation stripping concatenates words. blood."—The → bloodthe after re.sub(r"[^\w\s]", "", text). Should replace punctuation with a space, not nothing.

  ---
  Pattern of failures — one single cause across all books

  Every single "failure" is the same structural phenomenon: non-verse text appearing after the last verse of a chapter, before the next chapter's <sub>1</sub> marker. Because the checker's "verse
  range" is naively defined as text[sub_N.end : sub_N+1.start], any interstitial content after verse N but before the next chapter bleeds into verse N's word list. The actual verse words are always
  complete and correct; the problem is what trails after them.

  BOM (114 verses, ~2–37 extra words each)
  Plain-text section/book headers appear as bare prose outside <h1> tags. The BOM source file repeats chapter references like 1 Nephi 2 as untagged text before the <h1>1 Nephi 2</h1> heading, and
  book transitions ("The Second Book of Nephi", "An account of the death of Lehi…") appear as large prose paragraphs between books. These bleed into the last verse of each prior chapter/book.

  OT (117 verses, ~3–11 extra words each)
  Psalm superscriptions. The KJV places titles like "A Psalm of David, when he fled from Absalom his son" or "To the chief musician on Neginoth" as prose between chapters. These appear after the
  last verse of one Psalm and before <sub>1</sub> of the next.

  D&C (141 verses, ~3–89 extra words each — most severe)
  Section heading text. Each D&C section has a verbose header like "Section 2 — An extract from Joseph Smith's history relative to the coming forth of the Book of Mormon..." that appears between
  sections. These are long and account for the high word-diff count (9,870 total).

  PoGP (16 verses, 4 extra words each, perfectly consistent)
  Chapter dateline headers. Every chapter ends before a block like Moses 2 June-October 1830. Clean and mechanical — exactly 4 words every time.

  NT (0 verses affected)
  The NT source text has no interstitial plain-text content between chapters — chapter headings are cleanly in <h1> tags and no prose bridges chapters.

  ---
  Bottom line on actual scripture accuracy

  The scripture text itself is perfect across all five books. When punctuation is correctly replaced with spaces (so blood."—The doesn't concatenate), zero mid-verse word mismatches exist anywhere.
  The 388 "affected" verses across the four flagged books all pass cleanly once you scope the comparison to the actual verse word range rather than the full inter-<sub> range. The failures the
  thorough checker surfaces are real — they reflect structural text bleeding into verse ranges — but they are not accuracy problems with the scripture words themselves.
